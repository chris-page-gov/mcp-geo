Why it matters: VDSL2 (superfast broadband) performance degrades sharply beyond ~500m from cabinet. At 2,300m from the cabinet — and with the exchange itself 20km away — ADSL attenuation was so severe that Meeth fell completely outside the reach of any viable FTTC upgrade. Only a full FTTP deployment, bypassing copper entirely, could solve it. This is precisely the "distant exchange" classification that put Meeth into the hardest-to-reach BDUK tier.

📅 Meeth — Timeline
From clay railways to gigabit fibre
1800s

Ball Clay Mining Begins

Meeth's economy built around ball clay extraction — used in tiles, sanitaryware and ceramics. Shaft and tunnel methods employed across the parish.

1925

North Devon & Cornwall Junction Light Railway

Railway opens through Meeth (Halwill Junction to Torrington), giving the clay mines direct rail access to Bideford for sea transport.

1970s

Peak Clay Production

70,000 tonnes per year extracted. Nearly 50 local jobs. Meeth's own station served the works until 1981.

1982

Railway Closes

North Devon & Cornwall Junction Light Railway ceases operation. Meeth becomes more isolated.

2004

Ball Clay Mines Close

The mines that defined the parish for two centuries shut permanently. The 150-hectare site falls silent.

2006

Hatherleigh Exchange Gets ADSL Max

Broadband arrives — theoretically. But with 20 km of copper between Meeth and the exchange, practical speeds are 3–4 Mbps at best.

2010–2016

Connecting Devon & Somerset Phase 1 (CDS)

£94m programme delivers superfast broadband to 290,000+ Devon premises — but Meeth, classed as "too distant", is left out of the FTTC rollout.

2013

Meeth Quarry Becomes a Nature Reserve

Devon Wildlife Trust purchases the 150-hectare former clay site. Wetlands, heathland and woodland established. Home to marsh fritillary butterflies and Devon's largest Goosander roost.

2020–2024

CDS Phase 2 & Airband Fixed Wireless

Some rural areas get fixed-wireless 100 Mbps via Airband. Meeth receives partial coverage, subject to survey — a significant improvement but still not universal FTTP.

2024

BDUK Awards £800m Project Gigabit Contract to Openreach

Type C framework signed. Meeth falls into Call-Off 4 (Mid Devon, North Somerset, South Devon) — 37,000 premises, £77m public subsidy. The "hardest to reach" finally in scope.

Feb 2025

🎉 First Gigabit Connections Live in Meeth

Meeth becomes one of only 38 communities in the inaugural Project Gigabit FTTP wave. The Bull & Dragon pub (est. ~1490) goes from 3 Mbps to gigabit broadband. A village transformed.

"We were running on 3–4 Mbps. Video calls dropped constantly, and you couldn't run the booking system and the till at the same time. Now we've got full fibre — it's genuinely changed how we run the business."
— Mike Tunnicliffe, Landlord, The Bull & Dragon, Meeth (est. ~1490) · February 2025
📌 Meeth at a Glance
Key facts from OS Places, OS Names & parish records
🏘️

Settlement Character

A quiet rural village classified as a "Populated Place — Village" in the OS Names dataset (ID: osgb4000000074574126). Surrounded by farmland, with the River Torridge forming a natural western boundary.

⛪

Church of St Michael & All Angels

The parish church, recently re-opened after restoration works. The village also has a hall and the Bull & Dragon pub, one of North Devon's oldest, dating from approximately 1490.

🏞️

Meeth Quarry Nature Reserve

150-hectare former ball clay mine purchased by Devon Wildlife Trust in 2013. Now features wetlands, heathland and woodland — and Devon's largest Goosander roost (50+ birds). OS Names ID: osgb4000000074821139.

🚂

Lost Railway

The North Devon & Cornwall Junction Light Railway (1925–1982) ran through Meeth, serving the clay mines with a halt at "Meeth Halt". The trackbed is now a footpath and nature corridor.

🌊

River Torridge Confluence

Meeth sits close to the confluence of the Torridge and Okement rivers. The Torridge rises on Dartmoor and flows north through the parish before reaching the estuary at Bideford — made famous by Henry Williamson's Tarka the Otter.

🏺

Ball Clay Heritage

Ball clay — prized for its plasticity and pale-firing properties — was extracted here for nearly 200 years. Used in high-quality ceramics, tiles and sanitaryware. At peak: 70,000 tonnes/year, ~50 jobs. Closed 2004.

🇬🇧 Programme Context — Connecting Devon & Somerset / Project Gigabit
How Meeth fits into the national broadband story
📡

CDS Phase 1 (2010–2016)

£94m programme. Delivered FTTC superfast broadband (24Mbps+) to 290,000+ Devon premises. Meeth excluded — too distant from any viable FTTC cabinet.

🛜

CDS Phase 2 (2020–2024)

£80m FTTP programme via Airband, Truespeed & Wessex Internet. Partial fixed-wireless coverage for some Dartmoor/Exmoor communities including parts near Meeth.

🚀

Project Gigabit Type C (2024–)

£800m BDUK–Openreach framework. Type C targets premises with zero commercial interest. 297,000 hard-to-reach UK premises. Meeth: Call-Off 4 (£77m, 37,000 premises, Mid Devon).

🏆

"First 38" Communities

Meeth was one of only 38 communities chosen for the inaugural Project Gigabit FTTP wave in February 2025, marking the first Openreach intervention in the hardest-to-reach rural tier under the new programme.
