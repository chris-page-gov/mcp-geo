# MCP-Geo Phase 2 evidence-backed benchmark and positioning framework for blocked and hard-to-answer UK public-sector questions dependent on Ordnance Survey data

## Executive summary

**Evidence-backed findings (facts).** Phase 1 established a baseline inventory of **25** currently evidenced, operational UK public-sector question-types that are already being answered with Ordnance Survey data (notably around UPRN/address resolution, planning-policy mapping, routing/network analytics, flood/postcode lookups, and “filter/count/map buildings” use cases). It also surfaced concrete evidence gaps—especially around access-mode detail (API vs download vs enterprise cache), automation of downloads, and areas where operational teams depend on intermediaries. fileciteturn0file0

Phase 2 extends that baseline by identifying **blocked, partially answered, or hard-to-answer questions** where the friction is (explicitly) attributed to:
- **manual or time-consuming access and integration** of “raw” geospatial data in real workflows, including planning and housing contexts citeturn19view0
- **cross-organisation data format mismatch** that becomes a time cost in emergencies, plus the need to keep Ordnance Survey-derived holdings synchronised across partners citeturn23view0
- **address and identifier integration problems** (manual free-text addresses; missing UPRNs; re-keying; post-hoc matching) and the overhead of file-based address data loads versus API-based integration citeturn9view0turn36view0
- **network/routing workflows requiring specialist build steps** that can take days (for example, building a routable network in pgRouting) citeturn28view3
- **highways/statistical reporting workflows that still require manual returns or spreadsheets**, and active efforts to replace those manual processes using authoritative road geometry and identifiers citeturn28view0
- **licensing constraints** that complicate open publication of “address ↔ UPRN” pairings when both were sourced from licensed Ordnance Survey products (a blocker for open datasets and transparency use-cases). citeturn31view0
- **operational data collection and reporting practices still anchored in spreadsheets and manual checking**, even in nationally significant programmes that are UPRN- and AddressBase-dependent. citeturn11view0turn28view2

**Structured interpretation (inference).** Taken together, the evidence supports a benchmark and positioning framework where MCP-Geo is evaluated and positioned less as “a GIS replacement” and more as a **workflow accelerant** that:
- reduces dependency on scarce specialists for **repeatable, standard queries**, without attempting to replace deeper cartography/data engineering
- bridges the practical gap between **API-first** and **download/cache-first** OS consumption models (both patterns are evidenced as necessary, depending on service context) citeturn9view0turn18view0turn23view0
- operationalises the government requirement to use UPRN/USRN identifiers by making “identifier-first” location questions easier to ask, answer, verify, and audit. citeturn36view0turn23view0

## How Phase 2 builds on Phase 1

**Phase 1 baseline (facts).** Phase 1 catalogued confirmed, currently-in-use OS-dependent public-sector question patterns and access modes, including: (a) address/UPRN resolution, (b) postcode lookup services with caching patterns, (c) planning mapping use anchored in OS basemaps, (d) routing and network-constraint problems, and (e) building selection/counting tasks. fileciteturn0file0

**Phase 1 evidence gaps (facts).** Phase 1 also documented evidence gaps that are directly relevant to MCP-Geo evaluation design—especially where public descriptions omit whether consumption is via API, bulk resupply/download, vendor-managed supply chain, or enterprise GIS cache, and where automation of downloads wasn’t strongly evidenced in named public bodies. fileciteturn0file0

**Phase 2 extension approach (facts + inference).** Phase 2 treats Phase 1 “working use cases” as the **minimum viable reality check**, then looks “one step upstream” at:
- the **unmet question** (what users still can’t ask/answer quickly)
- the **workflow friction** (manual steps; specialist bottlenecks; stale copies; licensing constraints; weak discoverability)
- the **stakeholder group** that experiences (or absorbs) the friction
- whether MCP-Geo could plausibly improve the path from question → grounded answer, given the OS data dependency and access model.

## Method and evidence rules

**Evidence sources and search strategy (facts).** This phase prioritised publicly accessible, high-authority material aligned to the user’s required source types, specifically: government guidance and standards (including emergency planning and open standards), programme methodology documents, public code repositories used in government delivery, and public-sector geospatial partnership materials. citeturn23view0turn36view0turn11view0turn17view0turn17view1turn28view3

**Rules used to classify each case (facts).** Each blocked/hard-to-answer case is categorised as one of:
- **Explicitly stated blocked question** (a source directly states the question is hard/not possible, or states a direct barrier)
- **Explicitly stated workflow pain point** (a source states the workflow is manual/time-consuming/resource-intensive; the implied question is then normalised)
- **Inferred unmet question** (a source documents a cumbersome process; the likely “question the organisation needs answered” is inferred and labelled as such)

**Confidence scoring (facts).** Confidence scores (0–100) reflect:
- strength of linkage between the question and explicit evidence of difficulty
- strength of linkage to Ordnance Survey data/products (named explicitly vs inferred)
- clarity of stakeholder/team and the nature of the blocker
- reproducibility/verifiability potential for benchmark use.

## Questions public bodies appear to want to answer, but cannot yet answer well because OS data access or usability is a barrier

**Patterns in blocked or partially answered OS-dependent questions (facts).** Across the evidence base, repeated “hard-to-answer” patterns cluster into a small number of question families:

**Address and identifier integration as a prerequisite for almost everything else.** Government open standards require that systems storing/publishing property and street information use UPRN/USRN identifiers, and link them to definitive information in Ordnance Survey products (under public sector entitlement). citeturn36view0 Yet emergency guidance warns that managing multiple address forms incorrectly leads to duplication and real effort/resourcing impacts, and that Ordnance Survey product updates must be maintained to avoid version conflicts across users. citeturn23view0 The GeoPlace integration checklist further makes explicit that free-text/manual addresses are unverified and duplicative, and that file-based address data import is “complex”, with non-trivial overhead that APIs can reduce. citeturn9view0

**Cross-organisation crisis questions are slowed by format and version mismatch.** Cabinet Office emergency guidance explicitly states that data presentation and incompatible formats can become a barrier; it can be time-consuming to convert multi-source data into usable data, and software/compatibility issues can delay information sharing. citeturn23view0 Inference: many “urgent” location questions (who is affected, where are they, what support is needed) are answerable in principle but remain slow in practice because address/UPRN joins and boundary overlays cannot be performed quickly by non-specialists.

**OS supply and “local copy” handling creates data management friction that shows up as operational delay.** AddressBase supply is divided into non-geographic chunks (where nearby features can be in different volumes, and features from different geographies in the same volume), with update mechanisms (COU) that require careful replacement logic—especially for geographic chunked updates where tile replacement semantics apply. citeturn18view0 Inference: these mechanics create predictable failure modes: stale copies, partially updated holdings, and workflow gatekeeping by those who understand the supply mechanics.

**Network/routing questions are “answerable” but can be operationally blocked by build steps.** OS routing guidance explicitly states that loading data into pgRouting and building a network can be time consuming and can take 2–3 days, with a recommendation to build routines that run overnight. citeturn28view3 Inference: that timescale converts many desirable “operational” routing questions (school transport, inspections, waste, welfare checks) into specialist, scheduled exercises rather than interactive questions.

**Planning/housing workflows show both data interoperability problems and capacity constraints.** The planning and housing landscape review explicitly states that access to raw geospatial data in an efficient manner is often manual and time-consuming, and highlights software and system interoperability constraints that “lock” users into tools that can’t communicate. citeturn19view0 It also reports that a majority of local authorities interviewed cited lack of geospatial skills/resources as a top barrier, with recruitment/retention challenges compounding this. citeturn19view0

**Highways/statutory reporting and reclassification still contains manual returns and spreadsheets.** GeoPlace’s write-up of highways-related work describes piloting the use of detailed road geometry to identify maintainable segments **without requiring manual submissions**, and explicitly acknowledges the pain of reclassification spreadsheets. citeturn28view0

**Programme reporting still depends on spreadsheets and manual checking even when OS identifiers are central.** BDUK’s methodology documents explicitly state that supplier delivery management information is submitted as spreadsheets and that some intervention data is updated via delivery tracker spreadsheets “manually checked by analysts”; they also note UPRN-level data gaps that prevent exact reporting in some cases, requiring estimation assumptions. citeturn11view0 Separately, BDUK’s “premises in plans” user guide states the release is optimised for technical users (programming), may not suit Excel, and that missing UPRNs can be due to new builds not included in the AddressBase Premium epoch used. citeturn28view2

**Where current OS access models create friction (facts).** The evidence points to three recurring friction points that show up differently by context:

- **API-first (transactional) access** is repeatedly positioned as a way to avoid file-handling overhead (GeoPlace explicitly describes APIs removing overhead of obtaining files, loading, and validation). citeturn9view0
- **Download/cache-first (batch/enterprise) access** is structurally complex for some OS products (chunking, COU semantics, periodic updates). citeturn18view0turn23view0
- **Hybrid access patterns** arise where organisations need authoritative identifiers and addresses, but also need repeatable analytics on stable copies (for example, address matching pipelines that download and transform AddressBase Premium for matching, including version management and chunking for resource constraints). citeturn17view1

**Where specialist GIS bottlenecks appear most severe (facts).** Planning/housing evidence explicitly links capability gaps and interoperability constraints to barriers in local authority contexts. citeturn19view0 Local-government practice evidence shows GIS teams acting as intermediaries even for statutory or recurrent processes (for example, a county council process describes housing development data as having no single source and being checked/adjusted through GIS workflows and audit spreadsheets, including cross-checks against OS basemaps and LLPG signals). citeturn22view0

**Questions especially suitable for a conversational OS-backed interface (inference grounded in evidence).** The strongest candidates share four traits supported by the evidence base:
- they begin with a **natural-language location reference** (postcode, address, place name) that must be resolved to UPRN/geometry citeturn9view0turn36view0turn23view0
- they require a **repeatable, templated spatial operation** (point-in-polygon; within distance; intersects; nearest; count-by-area), rather than bespoke cartography citeturn23view0turn22view0turn20view0
- they are slowed by **manual integration steps** (spreadsheets, re-keying, mismatch, manual returns), not by the absence of any data at all citeturn11view0turn28view0turn30view0
- they need an **audit trail** and clear versioning/lineage (explicitly raised in emergency guidance and address ecosystem transformation narratives). citeturn23view0turn30view0

## How MCP-Geo should be positioned for different stakeholder groups

**Stakeholder mapping (facts + inference).** Government open standards explicitly call out likely user groups for property/street identifiers (publishers of government data, data scientists, analysts, developers). citeturn36view0 Planning/housing evidence shows many local authorities facing skills gaps and tool interoperability constraints. citeturn19view0 Emergency guidance highlights multi-agency conversion and versioning burdens. citeturn23view0 These sources, combined with operational code and pipeline evidence (Defra and MoJ), support a differentiated stakeholder map focused on **who asks**, **who answers**, and **who maintains** OS-dependent spatial capability. citeturn17view0turn17view1

**Differentiated value propositions (inference grounded in evidence).** MCP-Geo positioning should vary by stakeholder “job to be done”, because the evidence shows friction is not uniform:

- For **operational users and service teams**: “validated address/UPRN answers fast; fewer errors and duplicates” (strongly grounded in GeoPlace checklist and emergency guidance). citeturn9view0turn23view0
- For **GIS teams**: “reduce the queue by handling routine, well-scoped questions with auditability; keep specialists on complex work” (grounded in documented skills/capacity constraints and GIS gatekeeping patterns). citeturn19view0turn22view0
- For **data/platform teams**: “bridge API/download complexity; improve pipeline repeatability; make lineage/versioning explicit” (grounded in AddressBase supply mechanics and public pipelines). citeturn18view0turn17view1turn28view3
- For **emergency planning/resilience**: “join-ready datasets and rapid affected-premises answers; fewer format delays” (grounded in Cabinet Office guidance). citeturn23view0
- For **highways/statutory reporting**: “reduce spreadsheet/manual returns; data-quality feedback loops” (grounded in GeoPlace streets evidence). citeturn28view0
- For **programme/statistics teams**: “reduce spreadsheet bottlenecks; surface data gaps/unknowns; improve reproducibility” (grounded in BDUK methodology). citeturn11view0turn28view2

## Recommended first benchmark pack for MCP-Geo evaluation

**Benchmark design principles (facts + inference).** Evidence indicates that the highest-value benchmarks should:
- start from **UPRN/USRN standards compliance and operational use**, because government mandates identifiers and they underpin multi-dataset linkage citeturn36view0turn23view0
- include both **API-first** and **download/cache-first** patterns, because both are evidenced as necessary, and each has distinct friction modes (file overhead vs chunk/update semantics vs deployment burden) citeturn9view0turn18view0turn17view2
- test **reproducible spatial operations** (counts, joins, overlays) with explicit handling of “unknowns” and update cycles, mirroring how public reporting currently deals with data gaps and epoch lag citeturn11view0turn28view2
- include at least one “hard” benchmark that reflects **specialist time costs**, such as routing network build and reuse, because evidence explicitly flags multi-day build cycles. citeturn28view3

**Recommended benchmark suite (structure).** This report provides a **75-question test bank** (Table 3).
- Priority ranks **1–25** = **top 25 must-test questions** (high cross-sector value + strong OS dependency + high benchmark verifiability)
- Priority ranks **26–75** = next 50 strong candidates (valuable but either more context-dependent, higher governance burden, or harder to verify at scale).

## Risks, caveats, and evidence gaps

**Evidence gaps that remain material (facts).**
- **Access-mode detail is still often under-specified in public narratives**: many descriptions of outcomes do not confirm whether OS data was consumed via OS APIs, downloads/resupply, enterprise GIS cache, or vendor supply chain; Phase 1 explicitly recorded this as a core evidence gap. fileciteturn0file0
- **Licensing constraints can be a hard blocker in open publication contexts**, particularly where a dataset’s “address ↔ UPRN” pairing provenance traces to licensed OS products; this risk is explicitly described by respondents discussing the difficulty of adding UPRNs to open datasets under certain licensing conditions. citeturn31view0
- **Some high-value workflows are inseparable from information governance and privacy constraints**, especially where UPRN enables household-level linkage (emergency vulnerability, safeguarding, multi-agency support). Emergency guidance explicitly frames interoperability and UPRN use alongside data sharing agreements and format governance. citeturn23view0
- **Routing/network analytics carry unavoidable build-time and compute costs** when organisations are responsible for building their own routing networks, with multi-day build steps explicitly stated in OS guidance. citeturn28view3
- **Planning/housing evidence strongly signals skills gaps and interoperability constraints**, but does not always identify the precise OS dataset involved in each constrained decision workflow; OS dependence is sometimes foundational but implicit. citeturn19view0

**Risks for MCP-Geo evaluation design (inference grounded in evidence).**
- If benchmarks rely only on “easy” API lookups, evaluation will miss the hardest and most operationally costly friction documented in the evidence: chunk/update semantics, pipeline build steps, and spreadsheet-based reporting bottlenecks. citeturn18view0turn11view0turn28view3
- If benchmarks ignore governance and privacy, evaluation will not represent the real adoption constraints in emergency/resilience and cross-service household linkage contexts, where UPRN is powerful precisely because it enables linkage. citeturn23view0turn26view0
- If MCP-Geo is positioned as a broad “answer anything” geospatial assistant, stakeholders may expect it to adjudicate planning judgement or replace statutory processes; evidence suggests the strongest near-term positioning is **repeatable routine answers + auditability + guided workflows**, not replacing professional judgement. citeturn19view0turn28view0turn36view0
